Travel Budgeting

Overview
Role/Tools

This is the result of a 5 week four person team project and we shared the work of conducting need-finding research to narrow our design space evenly.
Tools: Paper prototypes, Interviews, Surveys, Observations, Affinity Diagram, Personas, Data model

Problem

College students do not have a good grasp on how to create a budget for traveling and how much their trips will cost. In addition, they are very money-conscious, actively looking for deals, but often do not know how to tell if they aregetting a good deal.

Solution

Our final solution comes in the form of a browser extension. After user’s input their travel budget, location and desired dates, we can show what the average costs of other people who went on similar trips were. As a browser extension, this information is accessible to the user irregardless of what travel planning platform they are on. This unifies all the aspects of travel planning. Then, as they plan their trip, they can add the costs in the extension and see how their budget is being used and if it is practical.

Challenges

Among the challenges we faced in this project, our decision to pivot, while critical to our solution, also meant we had do follow up interviews to delve in the direction we wanted to since our original interviews were insufficient. In addition, testing our prototype was difficult because we were giving our users imaginary trip prompts. People were not as invested as they would've been had it been a real trip. In addition, real trip planning is time intensive and it is possible people did not put in that amount of time for prototype testing.

Summary

To conduct needfinding research and narrow our problem space, we conducted secondary research, interviewed our target audience, observed people as they planned imaginary trips, and sent out a survey online

Interviews and Observations

We conducted 3 rounds of 28 need-finding interviews where we watched someone plan a trip and then intereviewed them afterwards. OUr initial round of interviews were focused on travel planning in general, then on budgeting for traveling and more follow ups on tools used for budgeting.

We then consolidated our data in the from of an affinity diagram and grouped them into similar categories.

To figure out where the complexity of travel planning came from, we created a data model to map out the steps we saw our interviewees taking in their travel planning.

Survey

Now that we had a narrower problem to look at, we sent out a survey to get some quantitative data to back up our qualitative data. Of our 83 responses, over 50% of students claimed to budget (compared to our observations where few of our interviewees budgeted). In addition, when asked how they budgeted, they focused on flights and housing but didn’t go much further than that.

Summary

From our needfinding research, we created personas to depict our target users and looked at our competitors before coming up with some potential solutions

Personas

We noticed three general types of travelers:
Nick: Poor Spontaneous Jobless Student Nick is a 19-year-old currently in college. He is interested in traveling internationally but does not have a very big budget, all he has is the money in his savings account. His money comes from saving up while he worked at a student job at the local dining halls for minimum pay. He is spontaneously looking into traveling within a few weeks. When traveling, his lack of money lends him to seek out local attractions. He likes to visit lesser-known/hole-in the wall places. As an English major, he hopes to write about his experiences and as a result wants an out-of-the-box only has certain time options to travel (ie. winter/spring/summer break)
Wyatt: Working Budget-minded College Graduate: Wyatt is a 23-year-old who has recently graduated college and has a stable middle-class income. He has been saving up money for a trip for awhile now but still has a tight budget. However, it is more flexible since he has a steady source of income. In addition, he has more flexibility on when to travel as long as he tells his boss. His focus has been on gaining a stable career and as result, he wants to go wild on his trip. In addition, he is hoping to use this trip as an opportunity to see his college friends. He wants to take this time to recuperate and relax from the daily 9-5 grind.
Jane: Parental Travel Fund: Jane is a 19-year-old college student whose parents value traveling a lot and are willing to fund any of her desires to travel. Her family is well-off and she is not particularly concerned about budgeting. However, she is still constrained to a reasonable budget amount, particularly since she is traveling with her friends who do have a budget. She wants to pack as much things to do in one trip and is looking forward to trying the main attractions at each location. She considers herself a foodie and as a result, food will be allotted a larger portion of her budget.

Initial Ideas

We had a brainstorming session and came up with around 20 ideas focusing on ways to help travelers budget better or save costs. From these twenty, we narrowed down to 2 ideas based on competitive analysis and other research. Our two solutions we began rapid protyping for were:
1. A budget-minded travel plan builder that would let users compare the cost they envisioned mentally to the cost you are accumulating as you travel plan. This would give users a literal visual of the mental cost they store int heir heads.
2. A total cost calendar similar to the flexible dates/low fare calendars one can find for flights but comparing estimated overall cost including transportation, hotels, and entertainment prices.

Summary

From our two ideas, we created paper prototypes and used wizard-of-oz to test with users.

Paper Prototypes and User Testing

Paper prototype 1: We created a budget tracker website paper prototype but upon testing, we found that many people would not be very willing to use a completely new website, so incorporating this budget visualizer with the websites that people currently use to book the parts of their trip would be the best option. For example, including this bar at the top of the page on Expedia as a Google Chrome extension. This led us to our second effort.
Paper prototype 2: Now that it was in the form of a chrome extension, our second round of testing revealed a need for the ability to adjust the budget while on the trip as well as needing an idea of what the budget breakdown looked like at a day-to-day estimate. Users also failed to place accurate budgets into place.
Paper Prototype 3: For our final prototype, we added in differing budget estimates for different levels of lifestyles.
