Application Redesign to Version 2.0

Role: Lead Designer
Timeline: 3 Months (2020)
Team: 3 Designers

I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study; images and text may have been generalized. The information in this case study is my own.

Product Opportunity

With the creation of a new internal design system and framework, I worked on redesigning our existing product to meet our new design specifications and iterate upon the existing platform.

Design Overview

I worked with two other UX designers on this 3 month project. I focused on leading design critiques and review sessions with internal stakeholders, working across teams to determine the scope of the MVP release and owning some of the specific designs of the overall redesign.

The user interface before getting redesigned

Key Use Cases

The initial scope of the application redesign was enormous. Research was conducted to distill the key use cases we wanted to resolve in the first launch.

Our modelling of projections did not match users' mental model.

Our initial display used a tabbing format to allow users to compare different scenarios. However, we saw user confusion. They did not understand why the values are changing and why not all of the values changed.

The application load time was not proportional to payoff for users.

In areas where our application takes a long time to load, users were dropping off. It was clear we needed to decrease the load time as well as improve our loading pattterns.

The primary dashboard was too cluttered

Because the existing visual hierarchy of the dashboard had many items on the same level, there was no clear indicator as to what users should be paying the most attention to. There was no differentiation between items that were prone to change and items that are seldom accessed.

Redefined Problem Statement

How can we simplify the cognitive load of our application for users?

Research Highlights

Research was conducted in the form of a card sort exercise, online surverys, and usability hotspot testing to inform the intial use cases listed above as well as to iterate into our final solution. Particularly, research informed the information architecture and subsequent site map of the new dashboard.

A diagram of the new layout, which simplified the experience and made a clearer separation between items that were typically static as opposed to changing content

Design Highlights

While there were many things we wanted to change in the new application, our key use cases allowed us to hone in on what we would be solving for in this launch. Not only did we make changes for this application, we also developed design system guidelines from these solutions.

Improved content through consistency

A content review was undertaken, reviewing the copy, such as help text and info buttons, leading to standardized key terminology to reduce the vocabulary on the page. Additionally, rules for displays of numbers were put in place, allowing for consistent expecatations of rounding and precision.

Engaged loading times

Taking notes from gaming loading screens, the existing loading screens were replaced with loading screens that gave users something to look at while they waited. Additionally, instead of constant loading, information was loaded on an as-needed basis.

Side-by-side comparisons

In the previous tabbing style, the two scenarios could not be seen side-by-side; users would need to memorize the differences themselves. By placing the two scenarios on the same page, users were better able to draw their own conclusions to pros and cons.

The redesigned dashboard

Conclusion

Not only does this new redesign make use of our new visual design library, it also puts into place user experience guidelines from content strategy to interaction design patterns. The simplified experience also provided a stronger case for our product's value add, leading to increased sales.

Redesigned Automations Suite

Team size: 1 Timeline: 8 months (2021) Role: Research & design

I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information in this case study; images and text may have been generalized. The information in this case study is my own.

Context

Summary

As the team’s first designer, I was offered a blank slate to reimagine automations in our platform. After extensive user research, I identified actionable next steps to improve the existing functionality and established a longer term product vision to guide future feature discovery.

Product Opportunity

Users set up automated jobs for everything from due date notifications to automated field updates. As different teams added new functionality to expand our jobs offering, the automated jobs flow became an unwieldy amalgamation of features that overwhelmed our users. Users were confused and intimidated by the crowded user interface. It was evident that as we added complexity to our automations capabilities, the current platform layout limited our options for feature development. We needed to re-align the existing pieces of the platform to be more cohesive and create a more scalable strategy that considered the future of automations.

Research

Methods

To learn more about jobs and the pain points of the current flow, I sent out surveys, conducted contextual inquiries, and reviewed automations-related customer data. I wanted to understand what pitfalls new users encountered when they started to set up their automated jobs and what tricks our veteran staff leveraged to resolve these issues. I also needed to understand the real world use cases, and how simple or complex these automated jobs could become.

Synthesis

  • The current creation flow to set up an automated job didn’t match users’ mental model. When watching new users, they struggled to understand what each step was asking for and where to click next. They were not able to quickly resolve any errors they encountered. The existing affordances were not clear to users, and even our power users sometimes misclicked during routine tasks.
  • Users needed an overview. Since you could only see one part of a job at one time, users were constantly clicking back and forth to reference information.
  • Our jobs were hacky. Our power users proudly introduced us to a series of workarounds to create automated jobs. While it worked, it meant our existing functionality wasn’t exactly doing what users needed.

Design Solution

Creation Flow

  • IA sidebar now displays the entire job in one view, allowing users to reference information from previous steps and easily understand what a job is supposed to do at one glance.
  • Conditions can now be added prior to saving a job, allowing users to create their jobs in the order that makes sense to them.
  • Operations have been atomized to be more distinct and allow for a future with multiple operations.

Refinements

As a flow where mis-steps can have egregious consequences and it is easy to lose user trust, details could not be overlooked.

  • Terminology was updated to be more user-friendly and less technical in nature so that users had a clear understanding of what was being automated.
  • More explicit warning and error messages provided users with necessary guidance in areas where users were particularly prone to error.
